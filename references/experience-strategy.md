# Experience Strategy

Use this module to define what the experience should accomplish, mean, and feel before designing its form.

## Outcome alignment

Connect four layers:

1. **User outcome:** progress the person is trying to make
2. **Business outcome:** sustainable value the organization needs
3. **Brand promise:** expectation the experience must reinforce
4. **Operational truth:** what people, policy, systems, and support can reliably deliver

A concept fails when one layer contradicts another. A “calm and effortless” promise cannot rely on a slow, opaque approval process; a “trusted” product cannot conceal consequences.

## Jobs and context

Describe:

- Functional job: task or progress
- Emotional job: how the user wants to feel or avoid feeling
- Social job: how the user wants to be perceived
- Trigger and frequency
- Environment, device, attention, urgency, ability, connectivity
- Current mental model and alternative
- Stakes and cost of error

Design for the situation, not a generic persona biography.

## Experience principles

Write three to five principles as decisions, not adjectives:

`[Principle name]: We [behavior] so that [outcome]. Prefer [choice] over [alternative]. This does not mean [misuse].`

Example:

`Confidence before speed: Show source, scope, and consequences before a high-impact action. Prefer a reviewable summary over one-click automation. This does not mean adding confirmation to harmless actions.`

Each principle must resolve future trade-offs.

## Journey and emotion arc

Map a specific actor and scope:

| Moment | Trigger/goal | Action/touchpoint | Thought/question | Emotion/confidence | Friction/risk | Evidence | Design response |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Mark:

- Entry expectation
- First proof of value
- Highest anxiety
- Point of commitment
- Error/recovery moment
- Completion/closure
- Return/re-engagement

Do not aim for maximum delight at every point. Appropriate targets include calm, control, momentum, trust, competence, relief, curiosity, or pride.

## Service blueprint

For cross-channel or operational experiences, add:

| User action | Frontstage touchpoint | Backstage process | System/data | Owner | Failure and recovery |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Use the blueprint to prevent UI promises that operations cannot keep.

## Information architecture

- Organize around user objects, tasks, and decisions, not company departments.
- Give each screen/page a primary question and primary action.
- Use a clear hierarchy: global navigation, local navigation, content, related/secondary actions.
- Make labels concrete, mutually distinguishable, and consistent with the user's vocabulary.
- Validate findability with tree testing or task-based navigation tests when risk is material.
