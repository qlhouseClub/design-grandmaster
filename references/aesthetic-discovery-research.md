# Aesthetic Discovery and Visual Research

Use this process before art direction when the outcome depends on a client's taste, a named period or culture, an unfamiliar visual language, a current trend, or exceptional visual quality. Research is not a pretext for collecting attractive images. It must change the direction space and make the eventual design more specific, defensible, and readable.

## 1. Research contract

Before searching, define:

- Decision the research must enable
- Audience, deliverable, platform, context, and desired emotional effect
- Client stakeholders and final decision authority
- Aesthetic ambition: correct, polished, distinctive, authored, or inevitable
- Known references, anti-references, existing brand artifacts, and category constraints
- Subject boundaries: period, geography, culture, medium, material, school, and terminology
- Whether the user wants historical fidelity, contemporary reinterpretation, cultural dialogue, trend relevance, or deliberate fiction
- Evidence date, deadline, available tools, budget, image-use rights, and specialist access

Research questions should be falsifiable enough to reject a direction. “Find inspiration” is not a research question. Better questions include:

- Which visual mechanisms make the reference field recognizable without relying on literal motifs?
- Which apparent qualities come from original craft, aging, restoration, photography, or modern reproduction?
- What does the client's preference actually attach to: order, color, material, status, emotion, familiarity, or cultural meaning?
- Which principles survive translation into a readable interactive product?
- What would turn respectful adaptation into costume, stereotype, or imitation?

## 2. Client aesthetic discovery

### Gather evidence

Use concrete artifacts before abstract discussion:

- The client's existing product, brand, environment, wardrobe, packaging, decks, photography, or previous design decisions when relevant
- Three to five admired references and the exact parts admired
- Three to five rejected references and the exact reason for rejection
- Category references the client considers credible, tired, too safe, or inappropriate
- Adjacent references from architecture, fashion, publishing, film, music, objects, games, hospitality, or art
- Pairwise choices that expose trade-offs: restrained/expressive, ordered/irregular, polished/raw, familiar/novel, quiet/status-signaling, timeless/current

Do not ask only “What style do you like?” Use prompts such as:

- What would you keep if the logo and color were removed?
- Which reference would you still choose if competitors copied its most obvious effect?
- What feels too designed, too ordinary, too foreign, too nostalgic, or too commercial?
- Where may the experience be challenging or surprising, and where must it remain familiar?
- Which reaction matters more: immediate attraction, trust, cultural depth, ease, status, warmth, or long-term recognition?
- What would make you reject an otherwise beautiful direction?

### Separate four voices

Never merge these without discussion:

1. Personal preference of the client
2. Desired identity of the brand or work
3. Audience expectation and comprehension
4. Category and platform conventions

Record alignment and conflict. A founder may personally love monochrome minimalism while the brand needs abundance and warmth. That is a design decision, not a contradiction to hide.

### Produce a taste profile

Summarize:

- Attraction triggers and rejection triggers
- Preferred compositional tension, density, color behavior, materiality, typography, image character, motion, and finish
- Appetite for novelty, ambiguity, ornament, cultural specificity, and visible craft
- Status language the client seeks or rejects
- Non-negotiables and negotiable preferences
- Areas where the client lacks vocabulary or has inconsistent signals
- Decision authority and how disagreements will be resolved
- Confidence level for each conclusion: direct evidence, repeated signal, tentative inference, or unknown

Do not reduce the profile to a style label. “Likes luxury” is weak; “prefers dark fields, precise editorial type, one highly crafted material moment, and rejects gold ornament or loud status codes” is actionable.

## 3. Scope and term disambiguation

Named aesthetics are often bundles of different histories. Before searching, split the term into testable scopes:

- Period and sub-period
- Place and cultural exchange routes
- Medium: mural, manuscript, textile, architecture, object, print, screen, or contemporary art
- Material and process
- Patronage and use context
- Original appearance versus current aged condition
- Restoration, reproduction, exhibition lighting, photography, and color-management effects
- Later revival, modern academic category, commercial reinterpretation, and social-media shorthand

Search the exact term, related historical terms, material terms, object types, institutions, and contested definitions. Record when experts use different language.

Do not treat a modern label as proof that historical makers used the same category. Do not treat one surviving region, cave, court, workshop, or social class as the whole era.

## 4. Source architecture

Build a field from several independent lanes.

| Lane | Preferred evidence | What it answers |
|---|---|---|
| Primary artifacts | museum object records, archives, digitized originals, archaeological reports, in-situ documentation | what actually survives and with what provenance |
| Institutional interpretation | museums, heritage institutes, conservation labs, curated exhibitions | chronology, context, restoration, terminology |
| Scholarship and technical analysis | peer-reviewed papers, books, pigment/material analysis, historical research | why it looks that way and what changed over time |
| Practitioner and material evidence | artist/process documentation, foundries, printers, type designers, craftspeople | how the visual effect is physically made |
| Contemporary production | recent live sites, identities, publications, fashion, exhibitions, games, interfaces | what the reference communicates now and where it is saturated |
| Adjacent fields | architecture, cinema, music, performance, landscape, food, objects | transferable principles outside the obvious category |
| Audience and cultural reception | interviews, criticism, community discussion, user evidence | likely recognition, sensitivity, and misreading |
| Counter-references | clichés, failed adaptations, category templates, competitor convergence | what the work must refuse |

For flagship or culturally specific work, use this as a starting envelope rather than a quota:

- At least three credible primary or institutional sources
- At least three scholarly or technical sources when historical or material claims matter
- Roughly 20–40 distinct visual artifacts across five or more lanes
- Several current production examples and several explicit counter-references

Continue beyond the envelope when new source clusters still change the grammar or expose contradictions. Stop when new evidence mostly repeats known mechanisms and no longer changes the risks or direction space.

Prefer source diversity over search-result volume. Ten reposts of the same restored image are one artifact, not ten sources.

## 5. Visual corpus and source ledger

### Record every consequential source

Capture:

- Source title, institution/creator, URL or archive identifier
- Publication or object date and date accessed
- Object, period, place, medium, material, and provenance when known
- Whether the image is original, detail, restoration, reconstruction, reproduction, exhibition photograph, or contemporary interpretation
- Rights or usage limitations
- What is directly visible
- What the source asserts
- What the designer infers
- What may transfer and what must not be copied

Use evidence labels:

- **F — sourced fact**
- **O — direct visual observation**
- **I — inference requiring judgment**
- **P — proposed contemporary translation**

Never present `I` or `P` as historical fact.

### Cluster the corpus

Tag and compare:

- Composition, scale, density, symmetry, framing, sequence, and negative space
- Typography or inscription, line, contour, and image-text relationship
- Palette structure, value range, color adjacency, light, metallic or mineral behavior
- Material grain, layering, edge quality, wear, irregularity, and production marks
- Figure, landscape, geometry, flora, fauna, architecture, pattern, and symbol
- Rhythm, implied motion, repetition, transition, and narrative order
- Emotional register, ritual/social function, power relationship, and worldview
- Recurrent mechanisms versus rare exceptions

The output is a pattern map, not a collage.

## 6. Analysis and synthesis

For every important finding, use this chain:

`evidence -> observation -> historical/material role -> present-day meaning -> transferable principle -> digital hypothesis -> risk -> validation`

Example:

| Evidence | Observation | Transferable principle | Digital hypothesis | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| narrative mural field | many scenes coexist in a continuous visual world | layered narrative without isolated cards | continuous sections with anchored navigation and controlled focal peaks | information overload |
| granular mineral surface | color has particle, depth, and changing reflectance | material color rather than flat RGB decoration | high-resolution authored texture in hero imagery and transitions | fake texture or poor performance |
| ornamental border | border organizes and consecrates the field | framing can signal chapter and hierarchy | sectional rails, dividers, or navigation boundaries | motif pasted around every component |

Distinguish:

- Enduring principle from fashionable surface
- Structure from ornament
- Original material effect from age damage
- Cultural meaning from contemporary viewer association
- Historical evidence from creative license
- Inspiration from identity theft or direct copying

## 7. Translation into a digital system

Translate each selected principle through all layers:

1. **Meaning:** what the principle communicates in this project
2. **Composition:** grid, hierarchy, density, pacing, focal structure
3. **Typography:** voice, script coverage, hierarchy, measure, numerals, readability
4. **Color and material:** semantic palette, contrast, texture scale, lighting behavior, fallback
5. **Image and icon:** subject, cropping, provenance, authorship, consistency
6. **Motion:** causal or narrative role, duration, continuity, reduced-motion equivalent
7. **Interaction:** affordance, feedback, state, control, recovery
8. **System:** tokens, components, patterns, templates, governance
9. **Production:** performance, licensing, content pipeline, craft method

Use a readability firewall:

- Decorative texture does not sit behind long-form text or critical controls without a controlled surface.
- Historical density does not remove information hierarchy.
- Period color does not override accessible contrast or semantic status color.
- Unfamiliar symbols do not replace labels for essential actions.
- Narrative motion does not block navigation or comprehension.
- Aged, distressed, or irregular surfaces remain authored assets, not universal component skins.
- Responsive layouts preserve the principle, not a screenshot's literal geometry.

The best translation often changes medium while preserving logic. A border may become navigation structure; mineral layering may become image depth and transition behavior; a narrative scroll may become paced section continuity. Literal costume is usually the weaker option.

## 8. Direction generation and client calibration

Generate three materially different directions from the same evidence field:

- One direction closest to the client's demonstrated taste
- One direction that protects the project/audience when client preference would create a mismatch
- One adjacent or counterintuitive direction that reveals a stronger interpretation

For each direction show:

- Thesis and anti-thesis
- Evidence clusters used and ignored
- Historical/cultural fidelity versus creative license
- Visual grammar and signature move
- Representative hero moment and mundane utility state
- What remains deliberately contemporary and plain
- Accessibility, performance, rights, production, and cultural risks
- How the direction could age or become clichéd

Calibrate with concrete comparisons, not “Do you like it?” Ask:

- Which direction feels most like the intended identity, not your personal taste?
- Which specific mechanism should be strengthened, kept, reduced, or removed?
- What feels authentic, theatrical, too literal, too familiar, or too foreign?
- Which risk is worth accepting?

Update the taste profile after every consequential decision. Client taste is learned through the project, not captured once in a questionnaire.

## 9. Worked routing example: Tang-period mineral-color website

The phrase “唐朝岩彩” is not yet a sufficient art direction. First split the research field:

1. Tang-period Dunhuang cave murals by cave, date, subject, patronage, and restoration state
2. Tang tomb murals in the Chang'an region, including architecture, figures, objects, daily life, and composition
3. Mineral and organic pigment evidence, binders, layering, aging, and conservation science
4. Tang-period textiles, metalwork, ceramics, architecture, calligraphy, and Silk Road exchange as adjacent evidence
5. Modern and contemporary Chinese rock-color painting as a later material/artistic discourse
6. Current websites, exhibitions, games, fashion, and brand adaptations that create contemporary audience associations
7. Counter-references: generic “national style,” Dunhuang clip-art, flying-apsara decoration, gold-particle templates, faux parchment, and indiscriminate saturated gradients

Useful starting sources include:

- Dunhuang Academy material on [cave architecture and the four exhibition color fields](https://www.dha.ac.cn/clzl/lnzl/qndh__skbhzdyzzm.htm) and its [Tang-period costume research exhibition](https://www.dha.ac.cn/clzl/lnzl/yyns__dhtdfswhjcxsjxsz.htm) for color, clothing, pattern, architecture, reconstruction, and contemporary adaptation evidence
- [Shaanxi History Museum's Tang mural collection](https://www.sxhm.com/tang_mural.html) for tomb mural subjects, architecture, figures, objects, daily life, and social context
- [Palace Museum research on Tang-period blue-green landscape in Dunhuang murals](https://www.dpm.org.cn/Uploads/File/2018/10/11/u5bbeec262a998.pdf) for composition, filled visual fields, and pigment use
- Scientific pigment studies for specific sites and objects, such as [Kizil mineral-pigment research](https://jogg.cug.edu.cn/cn/article/doi/10.15964/j.cnki.027jgg.2022.05.021) and [the Tang-period Murong Zhong tomb pigment analysis](https://qkzzs.jcu.edu.cn/info/1011/5876.htm)
- [China Academy of Art's Rock Color Painting Institute](https://www.caa.edu.cn/xy/xsjg/ychyjs.htm) for the contemporary field and its material discourse

Initial hypotheses must remain provisional until the corpus is built. Examples worth testing:

- Full-field narrative density may translate into continuous web chapters with clear focal peaks rather than disconnected card grids.
- Mineral granularity and layered color may become authored imagery, lighting, and transition behavior, not noisy texture behind every surface.
- Borders and architectural bands may become section framing or navigation structure rather than decorative frames around buttons.
- Rhythmic figures, clouds, ribbons, and repeated motifs may inform pacing and directional motion without copying recognizable figures.
- Stone blue, stone green, earth red, earth yellow, white, and dark contour relationships may inform a semantic palette, but sampled historical colors need provenance and accessible remapping.
- Current weathering and fading must not be presented as the original Tang palette; aged-surface and reconstructed-color approaches should be named separately.
- Contemporary “rock-color” materiality can be a dialogue with historical evidence, but it must not silently stand in for all Tang painting.

Only after client taste discovery and corpus synthesis should the designer choose among directions such as monumental narrative field, mineral-light material system, or restrained contemporary editorial interpretation. None is automatically “the Tang style.”

## 10. Output contract

For research-led art direction, deliver:

1. Client Aesthetic Profile
2. Research Contract and term/scope map
3. Search/query map and source ledger
4. Clustered visual corpus with provenance and rights notes
5. Pattern map and contradictions
6. Cultural, authenticity, imitation, and misreading risks
7. Evidence-to-digital translation matrix
8. Three art directions and aesthetic-jury decision
9. Client calibration record
10. Open questions, specialist-review needs, and next research trigger

## 11. Quality gates and stopping rules

- **Client gate:** preference is evidenced by examples, rejections, and trade-offs; personal taste is separated from brand and audience needs.
- **Scope gate:** ambiguous terms, period, place, medium, material, restoration, revival, and contemporary interpretation are separated.
- **Coverage gate:** primary/institutional, scholarly/technical, contemporary, adjacent, audience, and counter-reference lanes are represented as relevant.
- **Provenance gate:** consequential images and claims retain source, date, status, and uncertainty.
- **Saturation gate:** new research no longer changes the grammar, risks, or direction space.
- **Synthesis gate:** the work extracts mechanisms and contradictions rather than averaging a moodboard.
- **Translation gate:** every historical or trend signal has a meaningful digital role, boundary, and failure mode.
- **Readability gate:** content, interaction, accessibility, responsive behavior, and performance survive the visual language.
- **Originality gate:** the identity does not depend on one source, iconic artifact, artist, or recognizable motif.
- **Cultural gate:** cultural claims and sensitive symbols receive appropriate expert or stakeholder review.
- **Rights gate:** use of images, scans, fonts, artworks, and generated derivatives is documented and lawful.

Do not stop because the board looks full. Stop because the evidence is diverse, contradictions are understood, new sources have reached saturation, and the remaining uncertainty is explicitly bounded.
